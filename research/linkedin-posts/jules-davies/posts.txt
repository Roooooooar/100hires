POST 1:

If I were the Head of SEO at a B2B SaaS and wanted to show up in AI search, here's the exact playbook I'd run:

Step 1: Find where you're invisible

Before building anything, I'd run a full keyword gap analysis.

This is where I'd use Ahrefs Agent A. Give it your domain, your top competitors, your ICP. It surfaces every keyword gap, clusters them by intent, and flags which ones have AI search visibility potential. What used to take a full day of manual Ahrefs work now takes under an hour.

For most companies, this surfaces the same blind spot: comparison keywords, alternative pages, and use-case content.

Step 2: Fix your Google foundation first

AI search pulls from what already exists on the web. If your Google SEO is weak, your AI search visibility will be too.

→ Fix pages stuck on page 2
→ Reclaim broken backlinks pointing to 404s
→ Eliminate keyword cannibalization between thin pages
→ Clean up crawlability, indexation, and redirect chains

Step 3: Build content that targets the full funnel but start BOFU

Most SaaS content programs are top-heavy. Lots of awareness content, very little that catches buyers at the moment they're ready to decide.

I'd flip that. BOFU first (golden keywords highest LTV buyers look for):

→ "[Competitor] alternatives" pages
→ "Best [category] for [use case]" pages
→ "X vs Y" comparison pages
→ Switch-moment content — "migrating from [tool]"

These are the keywords buyers search right before they make a decision.

They're also the prompts they type into ChatGPT and Perplexity. Own these and you're showing on Google and in AI search simultaneously.

Step 4: Get cited in the sources AI actually pulls from

Publishing on your own domain isn't enough anymore. AI tools don't just crawl your blog. They pull from Reddit, YouTube, third-party listicles, G2 reviews, Wikipedia, and comparison sites.

So I'd backtrace sources AI pulls from for prompts my buyers type and build a presence across all of them.

Step 5: Analyze how you’re performing inside LLMs

Monitor your share of voice in LLM responses:

- Mention frequency: How often you appear in AI answers?
- Prompt quality: Are you showing up for buyer-intent queries or generic ones?
- Sentiment: Are LLMs positioning you positively, neutrally, or negatively?

POST 2:

Every CMO right now is asking one question: how do I show up in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini?

Before we get into that, let me break down how AI search works:

Every prompt you type into AI falls into one of two buckets:

1/ Informational intent

These are prompts like “how do I organize my email inbox?” or “how do I reduce churn?”

For these queries, AI looks for sources that clearly explain the topic and synthesizes an answer.

2/ Commercial intent

Think of prompts like “what’s the best CRM for startups” or “suggest 10 best email marketing platforms for ecommerce”

For these prompts, AI isn't looking for a single source. It's looking for consensus.

It scans:

Reddit threads
Third-party listicles
Product review sites
YouTube videos
Blog posts
Product pages
Wikipedia

Then it asks: "Which brands keep showing up across these sources?"

The brands that appear consistently across multiple trusted sources are the brands AI is most likely to recommend.

So if I wanted more visibility in AI search, I'd focus on 3 things:

✅ Show up on the sources AI cites

That means building visibility on Reddit, YouTube, review sites, industry blogs, comparison articles, and listicles.

✅ Build consensus

Don't rely on a single channel. You want dozens of independent sources talking about you.

✅ Own the right context

It's not enough to be mentioned. You need to be mentioned for the right use case, like “best project management software for agencies”

POST 3:

If I had $5K to get my brand cited in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini, here's how I'd prioritize which channels to invest my AEO budget in:

1/ Identify your core topic clusters first

Before you touch any tool, map the 5 problem spaces your ICP is actively asking AI about.

For example, if you sell project management software, your clusters might be:

- Managing cross-functional teams
- Client reporting and accountability
- Scaling operations without hiring

Every prompt you track should map to one of these.

2/ Build multiple prompts for each topic

Buyers phrase the same question differently depending on where they are in their journey. You need to cover that entire surface area.

For "client reporting and accountability," your prompts might be:

- "How do agencies keep clients updated on project status?"
- "Best project management tools for client-facing teams"
- "How do I reduce back-and-forth with clients on deliverables?"
- "Agency project management software vs spreadsheets"
- "What do clients actually want to see in status updates?"

3/ Group prompts into Monitors inside Promptwatch

Instead of getting lost in hundreds of individual prompts, analyze visibility and citations at the topic level.

To do that, create one Monitor for each topic cluster inside Promptwatch.

For example:

Monitor: Cold Email Software

Contains:
- "What's the best cold email software for a B2B SaaS?"
- "Which cold email platform has the highest deliverability?"
- "What are the best alternatives to Instantly?"
- "Which cold email software should a startup use?"
- "How do Smartlead and Instantly compare?"

4/ Reverse-engineer citation data

Look at every source AI systems cite across prompts within a monitor.

For example, after analyzing 30 prompts in a "Cold Email Software" monitor, you might find:

- 35% Reddit
- 25% Third-Party Listicles
- 20% YouTube
- 15% Wikipedia
- 5% Company blogs

5/ Allocate budget based on citation data

Now that you know the exact sources AI is pulling info from, create content for those channels.

That's how you use citation data to create an AEO strategy to show up in AI search.
